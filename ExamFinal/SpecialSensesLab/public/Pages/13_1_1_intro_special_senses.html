Week 13: Special Senses (eyes and ears)

An Introduction to the Anatomy of Sight and Hearing

The special senses include vision, hearing, smell and taste. However, this lab will cover only the foundational anatomy of the eye and the ear.

Just a reminder that as you use the menu to the left to see images of the anatomy, be sure to click on the name of a structure and then after studying those images, see if there is a little "+" sign to the right. This means there are substructures to learn for that anatomy and we don't want you to miss any of it.


Accessory Structures of the Eye

  • Eyebrows: An area of short hairs above each eye that follows the shape of the lower margin of the brow ridges. It appears that eyebrows serve two main functions. First, they do help prevent some sweat, water and debri from entering the eye socket. Second, they are important in facial expressions.
  • Palpebrae: Palpebrae comes from ancient Latin and refers to the eyelid and blinking or winking.
  • Palpebral Fissure: This is simply the area between the open eyelids.
  • Caruncle: The caruncle is a small red triangular portion of the inferior medial corner of the eye. This structure contains modified sebaceous and sweat glands that produce material that helps lubricate the eye.
  • Conjunctiva: The conjunctiva is a thin moist membrane that lines the inside of the eyelids and also covers the sclera. The cells that make up the conjunctiva secrete an aqueous substance that contains mucus and water. The conjunctiva is also important for lubricating the eye. You may have heard the term “pink eye” before. Generally this is referring to inflammation of the conjunctiva often caused by an infection.

Lacrimal Structures of the Eye

The lacrimal structures of the eye are structures that participate in the production and drainage of tears. Have you ever noticed that if you were crying and weeping very hard or long that you got a runny nose? You can blame the lacrimal structures for that.

  • Lacrimal Gland: Glandular tissue over the superior lateral eyeball that continually releases fluid which clenses, lubricates and protects the eye surface. These secretions are sometimes called tears.
  • Lacrimal Canaliculi: These are small channels that start in the upper and lower medial eyelid and drain fluid into the Lacimal Sac.
  • Lacrimal Sac: The upper dilated end of the nasolacrimal duct. This dilated area acts as a reservoir for tears that are just leaving the eye.
  • Nasolacrimal Duct: This structure carries tears from the lacrimal sac into the nasal cavity. It is also called the "tear duct".

Extrinsic Eye Muscles

The skeletal muscles called extrinsic eye muscles are under reflexive and voluntary control. Together they rotate the eye in its socket up / down; side to side; and can even cause the eye to spin.

  • Superior Rectus: This muscles lies at the top of the eyeball and has the function of moving the eye up such that the cornea and pupil are facing up.
  • Inferior Rectus: This muscle is situated toward the inferior eyeball and has the function of moving the eye down such that the cornea and pupil are facing down.
  • Medial Rectus: This muscle is found on the nasal side of the eyeball and it functions to adduct the eye or move it sideways such that the pupil and cornea would face towards the nose.
  • Lateral Rectus: This muscle lies along the lateral side of the eyeball and it functions to abduct the eye or move it sideways such that the pupil and cornea face away from the nose.
  • Superior Oblique: This muscle lies along the medial side of the eyeball and sends a tendon through a kind of pulley attachment in the upper medial part of the eye socket. This turns the direction of pull for this muscle and helps it spin the eye in a way that it is possible to look down and out.
  • Inferior Oblique: This muscle also comes from the medial side of the eyeball but sends a tendonous insertion to the lateral and posterior side of the eye. When it contract, the eyeball spins to look up and out.
  • Levator Palpebrae: The levator palpebrae is a muscle located in the upper eyelid. This muscle is responsible for elevating the upper eyelid. It inserts into the skin of the upper lid rather than having a bony attachment. This muscle is particularly important for allowing an unobstructed upward gaze by retracting the eyelid.

Eye Cavities

  • Anterior Chamber: This is the space found between the iris and the cornea.
  • Posterior Chamber: This the space anterior to the suspensory ligaments and lens but posterior or behind the iris.
  • Aqueous Humor: The anterior and posterior chambers are connected and the fluid in these chambers is the same fluid. It is called the aqueous humor. The word "Aqueous" refers to the watery nature of the fluid.
  • Vitreous Chamber: This a large cavity that makes up the majority of the eyeball. The vitreous chamber exists behind the lens and anterior to the retina.
  • Vitreous Humor: This the fluid in the vitreous chamber and is not watery but more gel like. The word vitreous means to be glass like.

Fibrous Tunic

Tunic refers to garment or layer. The eye has three main layers. The Fibrous Tunic is the outermost layer and contains the sclera and cornea both made of fibrous connective tissue. The Vascular Tunic is deep to the fibrous tunic and the Nervous Tunic is the deepest layer and is in contact with the fluids inside the eye.

  • Sclera: This a fibrous connective tissue layer that is found on the external eyeball. When you hear the phrase, dont shoot until you see the whites of their eyes, the whites of their eyes is referring to the sclera.
  • Cornea: This a thin transparent covering over the iris and pupil. You may have touched the cornea when putting on contact lenses.

Vascular Tunic

The vascular tunic is the middle layer of the eye. It is generally accepted that there are three parts to the vascular tunic which include the choroid, ciliary body and the iris. The lens and pupil are not directly part of the vascular tunic but we will consider these structures as being associated with the vascular tunic.

  • Choroid: The choroid is a vascular layer of the eye, so we find blood vessels in the choroid. The choroid also contains pigment that makes this layer dark. The dark color of the choroid helps keep light from reflecting around the globe of the eye. This makes vision sharper. FYI: the posterior part of the choroid in many animals contains pigments that give the choroid an iridescent appearance. This is called the tapetum lucidum (Latin for "shiny layer"). You may have noticed this if you shined your headlights onto a cat, dog or deer and the eyes seemed to glow.
  • Ciliary Body: This is a circular structure that is an extension of the iris. The ciliary body produces the fluid in the eye called the aqueous humor. It also contains the ciliary muscles and connects to the suspensory ligaments.
    • Ciliary Muscles: The ciliary muscles are "intrinsic muscles" of the eye. They do not help move the eye like the extrinsic muscles. Rather, the ciliary muscles encircle the lens and can exert more or less pull on the lens depending on how contracted the ciliary muscle is.
    • Suspensory Ligaments: These slender fibers controls the shape of the lens. The tension on the ligaments is related to how contracted the ring of ciliary muslce is. The regulation of the shape of the lens helps bend light in a way that we can focus on things we are looking at.
  • Iris: This a thin circular structure composed mostly of connective tissue and smooth muscle fibers. This structure controls the diameter of the pupil through relaxation and contraction of the muscle fibers. This structure is also highly pigmented which gives rise the many colors of human eyes.
  • Lens: This a transparent connective tissue structure that is oval shaped (thicker in the center than the edges). This structure becomes more flat when the suspensory ligaments are taught (the ring of ciliary muscle around them is relaxed). The lens becomes more oval when the suspensory ligaments are slack (the ring of ciliary muscle around them is contracted).
  • Pupil: The pupil is not really a structure, it is a hole. The pupil is the opening on the anterior eye that allows light to hit the lens.

Nervous Tunic

We generally refer to the nervous tunic as the "retina". The retina contains several specific regions.

  • Ora Serrata: This a serrated edge that marks the boundary between the retina and the ciliary body. This junction marks the transition from the non-photosensitive area of the retina to the more complex, multi-layered photosensitive region.
  • Macula Lutea: This is an oval shaped kind of yellow pigmented spot near the center of the posterior retina. The macaula lutea has a particularly high density of photoreceptors and is an area that we would focus light on if we want to see more detail. Also, because the lutea is yellow, it absorbs a lot of blue and ultraviolet light. This helps us see more clearly in bright sunlight.
  • Central Fovea (fovea centralis): Near the center of the macula lutea is a small pit called the fovea centralis or central fovea. This area contains the largest concentration of photosreceptors and light focused here will provide us with the most detailed sight of the thing we are looking at. For example, we would want to focus the light coming from anything we read on the fovea to have the sharpest focus of the letters.
  • Optic Disk: This is a yellow circular area on the posterior retina nasal side. This disk represents the convergence of neurons leaving the retina and entering the optic nerve. This particular location on the retina does not have photoreceptors and so light focused here will not be seen. This is called the blind spot.
  • Optic Nerve: This is the structure that contains the neurons leaving the retina and coursing towards the brain. The optic nerve courses from the back or posterior of the eye to the optic chiasma.

External or Outer Ear

The external ear refers to all the ear structures that can be seen on the lateral head as well as the ear canal and ear drum which can not be seen but are located inside the skull.

  • Pinna (Auricle): This is what we would traditionally call an ear. It is the cartilage and skin that forms the structure of the external ear.
  • Lobule: The lobule is traditionally known as the ear lobe.
  • Tragus: The Tragus is the cartilage projection just opposite and a bit superior from the ear lobe.
  • External Auditory (Acoustic) Meatus: "Meatus" is another word for canal and is the canal that one would put a Q-tip in to clean the ear (not a good idea by the way and highly discouraged by those who treat the ear).
  • Tympanic Membrane (Eardrum): The tympanic membrane or eardrum is a thin membrane at the end of the acoustic meatus and it vibrates when sound waves touch it.

Middle Ear (Tympanic Cavity)

The middle ear starts at the tympanic membrane and ends at the cochlea. The middle ear contains the ossicles or ear bones.

  • Eustachian (Auditory) Tube: This is a tube that links the nasopharynx to the middle ear. In an adult, this tube can be just under 1.5 inches long. The middle ear is generally filled with air. However, the air of the middle ear is not in direct contact with atmospheric air except via the Eustachian tube. Normally, this tube is collapsed and so a pressure differential can develop. The Eustachian tube can be influenced to open with chewing, swallowing and even yawning. This can help relieve a pressure differential and you have noticed this already when flying in an airplane. It can sometimes help to yawn or chew gum when taking off or landing.
  • Auditory Ossicles (Ear Bones): The auditory ossicles are three tiny bones that serve to transmit sounds from the air to a fluid fillled cochlea. The loss of one or more of the ossicles would create a moderate to severe hearing loss, but hearing would not be totally gone. The term "ossicle" literally means "tiny bone". From lateal to medial the ossicles are named Malleus, Incus and Stapes.
    • Malleus: The Malleus is attached to the tympanic membrane or eardrum and when this membrane vibrates it causes the Malleus to vibrate on the Incus.
    • Incus: The Incus is connected by its broad end to the Malleus and on its more slender end to the stapes. When the Malleus vibrates, the Incus receives this vibration and passes it onto the Stapes.
    • Stapes: The word Stapes literally means stirrup and refers to the shape of this bone which looks like a stirrup connected to a saddle. The stapes is the smallest of the ossicles and in fact is the smallest bone in the body. The Stapes connects to the Cochlea and is the structure that will create sound waves in the fluid filled inner ear.
  • Tensor Tympani Muscle: This is a muscle within the middle ear. It is located above the auditory tube and it connects to the Malleus. Its job is to put tension on the Malleus so that vibrations can be dampened when sounds are too loud.
  • Stapedius Muscle: This muscle is the smallest muscle in the human body. It comes from the petrous portion of the temporal bone and then inserts onto the posterior Stapes. Similar to the Tensor Tympani Muscle, the Stapedius Muscle can adjust tension on the stapes to stabilize it and help protect against damage to the Cochlea.
  • Fenestra Vestibuli (Oval Window): This is a membrane covered opening into the Cochlea. This is where the Stapes attaches.
  • Fenestra Cochlea (Round Window): This is also a membrane covered opening in the Cochlea. However, it vibrates in opposite phase to the oval window. This means that when the oval window membrane is pushed in, the round window membrane is bulged out. This flexibility of the round window allows for movement of the perilymph fluid inside the Cochlea which is necessary to trigger the very sensitive hair cells.

    • Internal (Inner) Ear

    The inner ear contains the cochlea, vestibulocochlear (auditory) nerve and the semicircular canals. The inner ear contains fluid that will trigger hair cells when it moves.

    • Cochlea: The Cochlea is a fluid filled spiral shaped structure that is encased in bone deep in the skull just beyond the Stapes. The function of the Cochlea is to participate in the process of auditory transduction. In other words, the Cochlea will transduce sound waves into electrical impulses that the brain can interpret as individual sound frequencies.
      • Scala Vestibuli: This is a duct within the Cochlea that is filled with a fluid called perilymph. In a cross section of the spiral extension of the Cochlea, the Scala Vestibuli is the superior duct. It is seperated brom the cochlear duct by Reissner's membrane and it will join the inferior duct or scala tympani at the portion of the cochlea called the helicotrema.
      • Vestibular Membrane: This membrane is also called "Reissner's membrane and it separates the cochlear duct from the vestibular duct.
      • Scala Tympani: This is also called the tympanic duct. It is seperated from the Cochlear Duct by the basilar membrane. This duct travels from the helicotrema where it joins the Scala Vestibuli to the round window.
      • Scala Media (Cochlear Duct): This is a fluid filled duct that sits between the scala vestibuli and the scala tymani. The fluid in this duct is not perilymph however. The fluid is called endolymph and differs in the concentration of electrolytes that are found in the perilymph of the surrounding ducts.
    • Organ of Corti (Spiral Organ): This structure is located withn the Cochlear Duct. This structure is responsible for the transduction of sound waves to electrical signals.
      • Basilar Membrane: This is a membrane that lies along the "floor" of the Cochlear Duct. It moves in resonance with sound wave frequencies. The area of basilar membrane that is sensitive to a sound wave frequency changes along the length of this membrane because its composition is that of a structure with graded mass and length. This allows this membrane to seperate which portion of the Cochlea will be most active depending on the sound wave frequencies being heard.
      • Tectorial Membrane: This membrane lies in the cochlear duct just above the bundles of hair cells. As the basilar membrane vibrates, it will cause the hair cells to bend against the tectorial membrane. In this way the tectorial membrane plays a role in the stimulation of hair cells.
      • Hair Cells: These cells are sensory cells that contain hairlike projections called sterocilia that are deformed (bent) by the sound wave vibrations. These vibrations cause a mechanical movement of the basilar membrane that lies below the hair cells. Hair cells that are stimulated in this fashion trigger an electrical impulse in neurons of the vestibulocochlear nerve that travel to the brain.
    • Vestibulochochlear (Auditory) Nerve: This is also known as cranial nerve number VIII. The neurons in this nerve are sometimes classified as cochlear fibers or vestibular fibers. The cochlear fibers travel to the temporal lobe where the sense of hearing is interpreted. The vestibular fibers are important for our balance as they help the brain understand how the head is moving with respect to our environment.
    • Semicircular Canals: The semicircular canals are positioned more or less in the three cardinal planes (frontal, sagittal and transverse). This how they can sense movement in in any direction. They are important for balance.
      • Lateral Semicircular Canal: This canal is also sometimes called the horizontal canal. This canal senses movemet of fluid with rotation of the head.
      • Superior Semicircular Canal: This canal runs from anterior to posterior and senses movement in the sagittal plane. For example, it would sense the movement of shaking your head "yes".
      • Posterior Semicircular Canal: As the name dictates, this canal sits most posteriorly and lies in a direction that would put it in the frontal or coronal plane. This canal would sense movement when touching ears to shoulders or doing something like a "cartwheel".
      • Ampullae: The ampullae is a dilation at one end of a semicircular canal. A patch of hair cells is found at the base of the ampulla and are responsible for sensing fluid movment. This movement is transduced into an electrical signal that will travel in the vestibular fibers of the vestibulocochlear nerve.
