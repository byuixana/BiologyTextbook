Muscles of the Hips and Legs


Muscles of the Hip

  • Tensor Fascia Latae: Originates from the iliac crest and inserts onto the iliotibial band and hence the name. Because it attaches to the iliotibial band, it helps with flexion, medial rotation, and abduction of the hip, and it helps with lateral rotation of the knee.
  • Gluteus Maximus: This is the most superficial of the gluteal muscles. It arises from the gluteal surface of the ilium, as well as the lumbar fascia and the sacrum. It inserts onto the gluteal tuberosity of the femur and causes external rotation and extension of the hip joint.
  • Gluteus Medius: Just deep to the maximus, this muscle also arises from the gluteal surface of the ilium, but it inserts onto the greater trochanter of the femur. The gluteus medius causes abduction of the hip. It also causes internal rotation and flextion of the hip, as well as external rotation and extension of the hip.
  • Gluteus Minimus: The deepest of the gluteal muscles, it also originates from the gluteal surface of the ilium and inserts onto the greater trochanter of the femur. It helps to cause abduction of the hip and medial rotation of the thigh.
  • Iliopsoas: The iliopsoas is actually made up of two muscles, the iliacus and the psoas major. These two muscles join together to form one muscle band at about the level of the inquinal ligament.
    • Psoas Major: Originating from the lower vertebral column, it inserts onto the lesser trochanter of the femur and causes flexion of the thigh and trunk, as well as lateral rotaion of the thigh.
    • Iliacus: This one originates from the iliac fossa and joins the psoas major to insert onto the lesser trochanter. It also causes flexion of the thigh and trunk.
  • Iliotibial Band (Tract): The iliotibial band is a fibrous tissue that runs along the lateral leg and helps reinforce the thigh muscles. If you stand up and put your weight on a straight leg, you can feel the stiff iliotibial band right along the lateral surface of your thigh. The iliotibial band is often called the IT band.
  • Piriformis: This is a small, flat muscle that originates from the sacrum and inserts onto the greater trochanter. It helps with external rotation of the thigh. The sciatic nerve comes out underneath this muscle.
  • Gemellus Superior and Gemellus InferiorThe gemelli muscles sit just inferior to the piriformis. They also lie above and below the tendon of the Obturator Internus tendon. Gemellus is derived from the term geminus which means twin or twins. These two muscles look similar and perform the same action. .
  • Obturator Internus The Obturator Internus muscle origniates from the borders of the obturator foramen that is on the medical side of the pelvic bone. This orgin would be viewed from within the pelvic cavity. The tendon from this muscle travels right between the Gemellus Superior and The Gemellus Inferior and then finally attaches on the medial side of the greater trochanter.
  • Obturator Externus The Obturator Externus is a triangular shaped muscle that originates from the borders of the obturator foramen located on the outside or lateral surface of the pelvic bone. This origin would be viewed fromthe anterior outside perspective of the obturator foramen. This muscle also inserts in a similar place as the rest of these deep hip muscles. It inserts on the greater trochanter of the femur.
  • Quadratus Femoris This muscle is a flat, quadrilateral or rectangular shaped muscle that is located on the deep posterior hip. It is inferior to the other deep hip muscle mentioned just above. It inserts along the intertrochanteric crest that extends inferior to the greater trochanter and the insertion of the other deep hip muscles just mentioned.

Muscles of the Thigh

  • Pectineus: This is a small, flat muscle found in the upper thigh. It extends from the superior pubic ramus to the pectineal line of the femur and causes flexion, adduction, and external and internal rotation of the thigh.
  • Adductor Brevis: A flat, triangular muscle in the inner thigh. It helps to adduct the thigh.
  • Adductor Longus: Long, fan-shaped muscle located in the medial thigh. Runs from the pubis to the linea aspera of the femur and adducts the thigh, as well as helps with thigh flexion and external rotation.
  • Adductor Magnus: This is a very large triangular muscle found in the medial thigh. It originates from the pubis and ischial tuberosity and inserts on the linea aspera and the adductor tubercle of the femur. Its action is, of course, to adduct the thigh, but it also helps with flexion and extension of the hip.
  • Gracilis: Gracilis means slender in Latin and it is named such because it is a long, thin, flat muscle in the medial thigh that originates from the ischiopubic ramus and inserts onto the medial tibia at what is called the pes anserine which means a goose foot in Latin. It helps to flex, medially rotate, and adduct the thigh.
  • Sartorius: The longest muscle in the human body, it originates from the anterior superior iliac spine and runs the length of the anterior thigh across to the medial thigh and inserts onto the medial tibia at the pes anserine. Because it extends from the ilium to the tibia and runs laterally to medially, it is able to cause flexion of the knee, but also flexion, abduction, and lateral rotation of the hip joint.
  • Hamstrings: The hamstrings are on the posterior thigh and are made up of three muscles. All hamstring muscles originate from the ischial tuberosity. They all help with hip extension and knee flexion. They are important for sprinting and aggressive running can injure them.
    • Biceps Femoris: Again, this muscle is named because it has two heads, a long head which is superficial and a short head which is deeper. It is the most lateral of the posterior thigh muscles and it inserts onto the head of the fibula where it causes flexion and lateral rotation of the knee joint.
    • Semitendinosus: This muscle is named because of its long tendon of insertion. It sits just medial to the biceps femoris and just superficial to the semimembranosus. It inserts onto the medial tibia at the pes anserine and causes flexion of the knee, as well as extension of the hip joint.
    • Semimembranosus: The most medial of the hamstring muscles, it is named because of its flat tendon of origin. It inserts onto the medial condyle of the tibia and causes flexion of the knee and extension of the hip joint.
  • Quadriceps Femoris: The quadriceps femoris muscles are on the anterior thigh. They are often called the quads. The term quad refers to the number 4 as there are 4 muscles that make up this group.
    • Rectus Femoris: This is the muscle right in the middle of the anterior thigh. It is the only quadriceps muscle that crosses the hip. It originates from the anterior inferior iliac spine and inserts into the quadriceps tendon. It causes knee extension as well as hip flexion.
    • Vastus Intermedius: This muscle is deep to the rectus femoris and can be hard to see unless the rectus is removed. It originates from the anterolateral shaft of the femur and inserts at the quadriceps tendon, causing extension of the knee joint.
    • Vastus Lateralis: It originates at the linea aspera of the femur and also inserts at the quadriceps tendon. It causes extension of the knee.
    • Vastus Medialis: Runs from the medial side of the femur to the quadriceps tendon and also extends the knee.

Muscles of the Lower Leg

It may help, when learning the muscles of the lower leg, to also break them up into compartments. In the forearm, we saw that the extensors were found in the posterior compartment and flexors were found in the anterior compartment. As we look at the leg, we are going to see that the opposite is true; we find flexors on the back of the leg, and we find extensors on the front.

Posterior Compartment

  • Tendo Calcaneus: The tendo calcaneus is also commonly called the Achilles tendon. This tendon is very strong and serves as the common attachment point for the gastrocnemius, plantaris, and soleus. After receiving all the muscle fibers from these three muscles, this tendon attaches to the posterior calcaneus where it pulls up and causes the front of the foot to drop (this motion is called plantar flexion).
  • Gastrocnemius: This muscle has a medial and a lateral head which originate from the medial and lateral condyle of the femur respectively. It causes plantar flexion as well as knee flexion.
  • Soleus: Originates from the soleal line of the tibia and helps with plantar flexion. It is a powerful and important antigravity muscle.
  • Plantaris: This is a small, thin muscle that actually has the longest tendon of any muscle in the human body. It contributes to plantar flexion and knee flexion.
  • Popliteus: This muscle originates from the posterior surface of the tibia and inserts onto the lateral epicondyle of the femur. It medially rotates the tibia on the femur when the femur is fixed (sitting), and laterally rotates the femur on the tibia when the tibia is fixed in standing.
  • Tibialis Posterior: This muscle originates from the tibia and fibula and inserts onto the navicular and medial cuneiform bones. It causes inversion of the foot and plantar flexion at the ankle.
  • Flexor Digitorum Longus: This muscle runs from the posterior surface of the tibia to the plantar surface of the distal phalanges of the four smaller toes, and it flexes those four toes.
  • Flexor Hallucis Longus: Hallucis means of the big toe in Latin. Therefore, this muscle extends from the fibula to the plantar surface of the distal phalanx of the big toe and it causes flexion of the big toe, as well as helping with plantar flexion.

Anterior Compartment

  • Tibialis Anterior: This muscle originates from the upper lateral surface of the tibia and inserts onto the medial cuneiform and the base of the first metatarsal bone. It causes dorsiflexion and inversion of the foot.
  • Extensor Digitorum Longus: Runs from the anterior lateral condyle of the tibia to the dorsal surface of the middle and distal phalanges of the lateral four digits. This muscle extends the toes and helps with dorsiflexion.
  • Extensor Hallucis Longus: Originates from the middle of the anterior fibula and inserts onto the dorsal side of the base of the distal phalanx of the big toe. This muscle extends the big toe and also helps with dorsiflexion.
  • Fibularis Tertius: Note that there are three fibularis muscles. These muscles can also be called peroneus instead of fibularis. Older anatomy text books use the term peroneus and newer text books use the term fibularis. The fibularis tertius extends from the distal anterior fibula to the dorsal surface of the fifth metatarsal. It helps with dorsiflexion and eversion of the foot. This fibularis muscle, although named because it originates from the lateral fibula bone, actually belongs to the anterior compartment because it runs anterior to the lateral malleolus.

Lateral Compartment

  • Fibularis Longus: This muscle originates from the proximal lateral surface of the fibula and inserts on the first metatarsal and medial cuneiform. It helps with plantar flexion and eversion.
  • Fibularis Brevis: This muscle runs from the distal lateral fibula to the fifth metatarsal where it also helps with plantar flexion and eversion.
