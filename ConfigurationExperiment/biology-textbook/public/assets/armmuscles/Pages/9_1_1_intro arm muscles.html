Muscles of the Arms


Muscles That Move the Humerus

  • Coracobrachialis: This is named because it extends from the coracoid process of the scapula to the shaft of the humerus. It is used in adduction and flexion of the arm at the shoulder joint.
  • Deltoid: This muscle is named because it is triangular like the Greek leter delta. It is the muscle that gives the shoulder its rounded contour and it helps with shoulder abduction, flexion, and extension.
  • Latissimus Dorsi: This is your "lat" muscle. It is a large back muscle that covers the middle and lower back. It inserts onto the shaft of the humerus and therefore connects the upper arm to the spine and the hip. It can adduct, extend, and internally rotate the arm, as well as it can serve as a powerful rotator of the trunk.
  • Pectoralis Major: The largest chest muscle that lies under the breast. Its primary functions are flexion, adduction, and internal rotation of the humerus.
  • Rotator Cuff: These four muscles work together to keep your humerus stable in the shoulder joint while providing you with your range of motion. They can be remembered as S-I-T-S.
    • Subscapularis: Sub means "under" and "scapularis" refers to the scapula, so the subscapularis is a large triangle-shaped muscle that sits in the subscapular fossa. It helps to internally rotate and adduct the humerus.
    • Infraspinatus: Infra means "below" and "spinatus" refers to the scapular spine, so the infraspinatus is a thick triangle-shaped muscle that sits below the scapular spine in the infraspinous fossa. It helps to externally rotate the humerus.
    • Teres Minor: Teres means "round," so the teres minor is a round muscle that extends from the lateral boarder of the scapula to the greater tubercle of the humerus. It helps to externally rotate the humerus.
    • Supraspinatus: Supra means "above" and "spinatus" again refers to the scapular spine, so the supraspinatus originates from the supraspinous fossa and inserts on the greater tubercule of the humerus. It helps to abduct the humerus at the shoulder.
  • Teres Major: Just like the teres minor, this muscle is round and it extends from the lateral boarder of the scapula and inserts on the humerus, helping with adduction and internal rotation of the humerus.

Muscles Acting on the Forearm

  • Biceps Brachii: Named because of its two heads, a long head and a short head, this is the large upper arm muscle that you see when you flex your arms. It flexes the elbow, flexes and abducts at the shoulder, and can supinate the radioulnar joint in the forearm.
  • Brachialis: This muscle is in the upper arm, deeper than the biceps, and it helps to flex the elbow.
  • Brachioradialis: Named because it originates from the humerus and inserts onto the radius. It forms the lateral boarder of the cubital fossa and it helps to flex the elbow.
  • Triceps Brachii: This is the large muscle that runs along the back of the humerus. It is named because of its three heads; the long head, the lateral head, and the medial head. This muscle extends the forearm, as well as extends and adducts the upper arm.

Muscles That Move the Hand

There are a lot of muslces found in the forearm that act on the hand. It can be a little easier to learn all of these muscles if you break them up into the anterior and the posterior compartments. In the forearm, flexors mostly originate from the medial epicondyle of the humerus and we find them in the anterior compartment, and the extensors usually originate from the lateral epicondyle of the humerus and we find them in the posterior compartment.

Anterior Compartment

  • Flexor Carpi Ulnaris: Carpi means "wrist" and "ulnaris" refers to the ulna bone, so this muscle inserts onto the wrist bones on the pinky side (where the ulna is). This muscle does flexion and adduction at the wrist.
  • Flexor Carpi Radialis: This muscle inserts on the wrist bones on the thumb side (where the radius is) and causes flexion and abduction at the wrist.
  • Flexor Digitorum Superficialis: This muscle splits into four tendons that travel through the carpal tunnel and insert onto the middle phalanges of the four medial fingers. This muscle flexes these fingers and also flexes the wrist.
  • Flexor Digitorum Profundus: This flexor originates from the ulna and the interosseous membrane. It then splits into four tendons and inserts onto the distal phalanges of the same four fingers as the superficialis. It also helps to flex the fingers and the wrist.
  • Flexor Pollicis Longus: Pollicis means "thumb." This muscle originates from the radius and interosseous membrane and inserts onto the base of the distal phalanx of the thumb. It flexes the thumb.
  • Palmaris Longus: Inserts on the flexor retinaculum of the wrist. This muscle is believed to have helped with grip and hanging in our ancestors, but it is actually absent in 10-15% of people and its absence has no effect on grip strength.
  • Pronator Teres: Inserts on the lateral side of the radius. It forms the medial boarder of the cubital fossa and it causes pronation of the forearm.

Posterior Compartment

  • Extensor Carpi Radialis Longus and Brevis: These muscles insert on the second and third metacarpal bones and cause extension and abduction of the wrist.
  • Extensor Digitorum: This muscle splits into four tendons and inserts onto the extensor hood of each of the four medial fingers where it causes extension of those fingers.
  • Extensor Digiti Minimi: Inserts on the extensor hood of the little finger and causes extension of the little finger as well as helps with extension of the wrist.
  • Extensor Carpi Ulnaris: Inserts to the base of the fifth metacarpal and causes extension and adduction of the wrist.
  • Extensor Pollicis Brevis: Originates from the posterior radius and interosseous membrane, and inserts on the proximal phalanx of the thumb where it causes extension of the thumb.
  • Extensor Pollicis Longus: The longus originates from the posterior ulna and interosseous membrane, and inserts on the distal phalanx of the thumb, also causing it to extend.
  • Supinator: Inserts onto the posterior surface of the radius and causes supination of the forearm.
  • Abductor Pollicis Longus: Originates from the interosseous membrane and the posterior surfaces of the radius and ulna, and inserts on the lateral side of the base of the first metacarpal, causing abduction of the thumb.
