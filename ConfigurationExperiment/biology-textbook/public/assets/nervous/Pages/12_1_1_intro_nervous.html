Module 9: Nervous System

An Introduction to the Anatomy of the Nervous System

The nervous system can be divided into two parts: 1) the central nervous system, which includes the brain and spinal cord, and 2) the peripheral nervous system, which is made up of nerves and ganglia. The nerves in the peripheral nervous system are classified according to their origin. Cranial nerves arise directly from the brain while spinal nerves arise from the spinal cord. In this lab, you will learn to identify various components of both the central and peripheral nervous system.

Coverings of the Brain and Brain Blood Vessels

  • Meninges: Meninges is a word that comes from ancient Greek and it means membranes. The meninges consist of three different membranes.
    • Dura Mater: Dura means durable or tough and mater means mother. The dura mater is a meningeal layer around the brain that is made of dense connective tissue and helps contain and protect the soft tissues and fluids around the brain.
    • Arachnoid Mater: Arachnoid means spider and refers to the spider web looking processes that extend from the arachnoid mater to the pia mater. Look for these in some of the pictures.
    • Pia Mater: Pia means tender and refers to the very delicate membrane that lies right up against the brain's surface.
  • Falx Cerebri and Cerebelli: Falx means sickle and refers to the shape of the dura mater as it divides the hemispheres of the cerebrum and the cerebellum. Note that the falx cerebelli is not nearly as large as the falx cerebri.
  • Tentorium Cerebelli: Tentorium refers to a tent like structure. The tentorium cerebelli is a tent like membrane the covers the superior surface of the cerebellum.
  • Dural Sinus: The dural sinus is an opening in the superior portion of the dura mater. This opening collects the venous blood from the brain and drains it down the posterior skull until it enters the jugular veins that exit the skull.
  • Vertebral Arteries: The vertebral arteries travel through the transverse foramina of the cervical vertebrae and enter the skull through the foramen magnum where they join the basilar artery.
  • Basilar Artery: The basilar artery is part of the posterior cerebral circulation. It arises from the confluence of the left and right vertebral arteries at the base of the pons as they rise towards the base of the brain.
  • Internal Carotid Arteries: The internal carotid arteries come off the carotid arteries of the anterior neck and enter the skull through the carotid canals.
  • Arterial Circle (Circle of Willis): The arterial circle or circle of Willis is a rare complete circle. Arteries branch off the circle and feed the brain. This circular structure allows for blood to get to both sides of the brain even if a vertebral or internal carotid artery blood supply is compromised on one side.

Ventricles of the Brain

The ventricles of the brain are basically hollow areas deep in the brain tissue that contain cerebral spinal fluid, often called CSF. CSF helps float the brain.

  • Lateral Ventricles: The lateral ventricles are the two largest ventricles of the brain. Each cerebral hemisphere contains a lateral ventricle, known as the left or right ventricle, respectively.
  • Third Ventricle: This ventricle is found in the diencephalon.
  • Fourth Ventricle: CSF leaves the fourth ventricle to make its way around the brain between the pia and arachnoid mater.
  • Cerebral Aqueduct: This allows CSF to flow between the third and fourth ventricles.
  • Choroid Plexus: The choroid plexus is a secretory tissue found in each ventricle of the brain, and its main function is to produce CSF.
  • Septum Pellucidum: The septum pellucidum is a membrane that separates the lateral ventricles. It is most easy to view in a medial view of the brain that has been separated in two parts via a sagittal section.

Structures of the Cerebrum and Cerebellum

The cerebrum refers to the part of the brain that contains the cerebral cortex. The cerebral cortex is the outermost layer of brain neural tissue. The cerebral cortex is made up of gyri and sulci. A fissure is a deep division that separates different lobes or hemispheres. The cerebrum has two hemispheres. The cerebellum has two hemispheres that are joined together and not separated.

Cerebrum

  • Cerebral Hemispheres: The cerebrum of the brain is separated into the left and the right hemisphere. The right hemisphere controls the left side of the body, and the left hemisphere controls the right side of the body.
  • Sulcus: A sulcus is a more shallow kind of valley in the cerebral cortex.
  • Gyrus: A gyrus is the opposite of a valley and refers to a ridge on the cerebral cortex.
  • Gray Matter: Gray matter contains most of the brain's neuronal cell bodies.
  • White Matter: White matter is where the axons of nerves travel in the brain.
  • Longitudinal Fissure: This is a deep groove that divides the left and right hemispheres.
  • Lateral Sulcus (Fissure): A deep fissure in each hemisphere of the cerebrum that separates the temporal lobe from the parietal and occipital lobes.
  • Parieto-occipital Sulcus: This sulcus separates the parietal and the occipital lobes.
  • Frontal Lobe: Located directly behind the forehead, the frontal lobe is responsible for executive function and declarative memory.
  • Precentral Gyrus: Forms the posterior border of the frontal lobe and contains the primary motor cortex.
  • Central Sulcus: This sulcus is just posterior to the precentral gyrus (hence the name) and it divides the precentral and postcentral gyri.
  • Postcentral Gyrus: This gyrus is directly posterior to the central sulcus and contains the primary somatosensory cortex. It also forms the anterior border of the parietal lobe.
  • Parietal Lobe: Sits in about the middle of the cerebrum and is responsible for integrating sensory information.
  • Temporal Lobe: This lobe is found on the lateral cerebrum behind the temporal bones (again, the name really gives away its location) and is responsible for memory and emotion, as well as visual, auditory, and language processing.
  • Occipital Lobe: The occipital lobe is the most posterior lobe of the cerebrum and sits at the back of the head. It contains the primary visual cortex.

Cerebellum

  • Vermis: This is the neural structure that is found at the point of union for the cerebellar hemispheres.
  • Cerebellar Hemispheres: The cerebellum has two hemispheres that are actually joined together and not separated like the cerebral hemispheres.
  • Arbor Vitae: This is the cerebellar white matter, and it is named due to its tree-like appearance (arbor). It brings sensory and motor information to and from the cerebellum.

Corpus Callosum

The corpus callosum is composed of about 200 million heavily myelinated nerve fibers that connect the left and right cerebral hemispheres, allowing for communication between the left and right sides of the brain.

  • Genu: "Genu" refers to the knee or a structure resembling a knee. The genu is bend of the anterior corpus callosum.
  • Splenium: This is the thickest and most posterior portion of the corpus callosum and it connects the left and right sides of the temporal, posterior parietal, and occipital cortices.

Diencephalon

Diencephalon means between brains. This region is an area that lies between the cerebrum and the brain stem.

  • Thalamus: This is an oval structure of gray matter on both sides and connected to each other by a small band of white matter called the intermediate mass.
  • Hypothalamus: The hypothalamus is located below the thalamus and contains several regions of gray matter, one of which protrudes and is named the mammillary body.
  • Fornix: A band of white matter that carries axons to the hypothalamus from other areas of the brain.
  • Pineal Body: The pineal body is a neural tissue that secretes the hormone melatonin.
  • Pituitary Gland: This gland hangs by an infundibulum below the hypothalamus and communicates with the hypothalamus. These communications are crucial for regulation of the production and release of several different hormones.

Brain Stem

The brain stem is below the diencephalon but is still found within the skull. Below the foramen magnum, we call the descending neural tissue spinal cord. The brain stem looks much like a spinal cord at its most inferior end, but has some thickenings superiorly.

  • Medulla Oblongata: This is a thickening in the middle of the brain stem that contains nuclei gray matter important for control of vital body functions.
  • Pons: The pons is another thickening on the brain stem that protrudes anteriorly. The pons contains nuclei important for motor control.
  • Midbrain: The very superior end of the brain stem contains an area called the midbrain. The midbrain contains some nuclei called the corpora quadrigemina. The density of cell bodies in this area causes some protrusions that can be identified on the posterior mid brain. There are four protrusions called the inferior and superior colliculi, but altogether we call these the corpora quadrigemina.

Cranial Nerves

Even though cranial nerves come off of the brain stem which is in the skull, they are actually peripheral nerves and part of the peripheral nervous system. Students should know the name and the roman numeral for each cranial nerve. Some students have used mnemonic devices to memorize the cranial nerves. The following mnemonic may help:

"On On On They Traveled And Found Voldemort Guarding Very Ancient Horcruxes"

Or for those who like animals:

"Oh, Oh, Oh, To Touch And Feel Very Grateful Very Appreciative Horses"
  • Olfactory Nerve (I): The olfactory nerve has two parts, the bulb which is the very end and is more swollen than the tract which is a band of axons that connect the bulb to the brain.
  • Optic Nerve (II): The optic nerve is the band of axons that connect the chiasma to the eyes. If the eyes have been removed from the specimen or model, the optic nerve will often show up as only small residual nerves coming off of the chiasma. The chiasma is where the optic nerves join and allow some axons to cross to the other side of the brain. The optic tract is the band of axons that carry axons into the brain after the chiasma. Chiasma means "to cross."
  • Oculomotor Nerve (III): Eye movement and pupil constriction. Helps to focus the eyes and position the upper eyelids.
  • Trochlear Nerve (IV): Eye movement. Allows for movement of the superior oblique muscle of the eye. This makes it possible for you to look down as well as move your eyes towards and away from your nose.
  • Trigeminal Nerve (V): Motor and sensory tracts to and from the face.
  • Abducens Nerve (VI): Eye movement, abduction of the eyes.
  • Facial Nerve (VII): Facial expression as well as taste from the anterior 2/3 of the tongue.
  • Vestibulocochlear Nerve (VIII): Responsible for hearing, balance, and equilibrium.
  • Glossopharyngeal Nerve (IX): Motor and sensory tracts to and from the mouth and throat, as well as taste from the posterior 1/3 of the tongue.
  • Vagus Nerve (X): The main parasympathetic innervator of the viscera. Also contains pharyngeal motor tracts.
  • Accessory Nerve (XI): Controls the sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles.
  • Hypoglossal Nerve (XII): Controls the tongue muscles.

Review of the Spinal Cord

The spinal cord is still part of the central nervous system along with all the structures discussed so far in the brain. Here is a review of the things that you should remember about the spinal cord.

  • Gray Matter: Generally, this is talking about an area of cell bodies within the central nervous system.
  • White Matter: This is where messages pass between the gray matter of the CNS. It appears white because it is made up of myelinated axons.
  • Gray Commissure: A bundle of nerve fibers that surrounds the central canal of the spinal cord and connects the two halves of the spinal cord together.
  • Horns: These are projections of gray matter in the spinal cord and they consist of the dorsal horn, which is where we find sensory synapses, the lateral horn, which is where we find the cell bodies of the neurons of the sympathetic nervous system, and the ventral horn, where we find cell bodies of alpha motor neurons, which innervate skeletal muscles.
  • Columns: The columns in the spinal cord are areas of nerve cell axons and can also be referred to collectively as white matter.
  • Central Canal: The central canal is continuous with the fourth ventricle in the brain and it contains CSF.
  • Anterior Median Fissure: A division in the spinal cord where the anterior spinal artery sits.
  • Posterior Median Sulcus: Not an actual division of the spinal cord, rather it is the posterior end of the posterior median septum.
  • Dorsal Root Ganglion: This structure is near the spinal cord but not actually part of the spinal cord. It is a collection of cell bodies that are part of the peripheral nervous system.

Anatomy of a Neuron

  • Soma: Soma means body and refers to the part of a neuron that contains the nucleus of the cell.
  • Axon: The axon protrudes from the soma and can travel for considerable distance before synapsing with another neuron.
  • Axon Hillock: The axon hillock is the area of narrowing as the soma gives rise to an axon. This is where action potentials are initiated.
  • Dendrites: Extensions of the neuron that receive signals from other nerve cells.
  • Nucleus: Hopefully you remember what a nucleus is.
  • Myelin Sheath and Schwann Cell: Axons may be covered with a myelin sheath which is the cylindrical wrapping of a cell membrane belonging to yet another cell. In the peripheral nervous system myelin originates from a cell called a Schwann cell (in the centeral nervous system the cell that makes myelin is called an oligodendrocyte).
  • Node of Ranvier: A node of Ranvier is the bare axon region that exists between Schwann cells.
