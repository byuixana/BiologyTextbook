Muscles of the Head and Trunk

Most skeletal muscles connect from one bone to another (although there are a couple of muscles in the face and neck area that do not attach to bones). So, muscles generally cross a joint. Synovial joints are the most common type of joint to be crossed so that when a muscle contracts or shortens, movement occurs at the joint. Here are a few things that we hope will help you in your learning of muscle anatomy.

Learning muscle names can be easier if you realize that muscles are generally named because of one or more of the following descriptive concepts:

  • Location: For example, pectoralis means chest, so a muscle with this name would probably be found in the chest.
  • Size: For example, maximus means large and brevis means short.
  • Shape: The deltoid is triangular and the quadratus is square.
  • Orientation of the muscle fiber: For example, the rectus is straight and the oblique is a muscle where fibers run at an oblique angle to the body.
  • Origin and insertion: For example the sternocleidomastoid gives the name of the origin and then insertion in the name.
  • Number of heads: Biceps means two heads, triceps means three heads, so what do you think quadriceps means?
  • Function: An abductor abducts and an adductor adducts. A masseter helps us masticate or chew our food.

Head


Muscles of the Face

  • Buccinator: This muscle compresses the cheeks against the teeth for things such as blowing. It also assits in chewing.
  • Corrugator Supercilli: Pulls the eyebrows down and towards the middle, causing verticle wrinkles in the forehead.
  • Depressor Anguli Oris: This muscle depresses the angles (corners) of the mouth and helps with frowning.
  • Depressor Labii Inferioris: Depresses the lower (inferior) lip inferolaterally.
  • Levator Labii Superioris: Elevates the upper (superior) lip, helping to produce a variety of facial expressions, such as smiling or disdain.
  • Mentalis: The primary muscle of the lower lip. It helps express doubt and contempt.
  • Nasalis: Compresses the the nasal cartilages. It can flare the nostrils and some people can use this muscle to close the nostrils to keep water out when underwater.
  • Occipitofrontalis: A long a wide muscle of the scalp. It spans from the eyebrows to the superior nuchal lines of the occipital bones and it has two parts or bellies to it.
    • Frontalis: Elevates the eyebrows and wrinkles the forehead skin, which happens on the FRONT of the head.
    • Occipitalis: Retracts the scalp, pulls the scalp back towards the occipital bones on the back of the head.
    • Epicranial (Galea) Aponeurosis: Aponeurosis is a dense connective tissue. "Epi-" means above and "cranial" refers to the cranuim, which are the 8 bones that protect the brain. So, the epicranial aponeurosis is a connective tissue that sits above the cranuim, or on top of the head.
  • Orbicularis Oculi: "Orbicularis" refers to a muscle surrounding an oppening and "oculi" refers to the eye, so this is a muscle that surrounds the eye. It causes the eyelids to close when it contracts.
  • Orbicularis Oris: Oris means "mouth" or "oral cavity" in Greek. This muscle surrounds the mouth and works with the buccinator to do things like sucking, chewing, whistling, and kissing.
  • Platysma: Covers the sternocleidomastoid. Produces slight wrinkles in the neck and draws the corners of the mouth inferiorly.
  • Risorius: Riso means "laugh" or refers to laughter in the latin languages, so this muscle helps to produce a smile.
  • Zygomaticus Major: Originates from the zygomatic bone and extends to the angle of the mouth. Its main action is to pull the corners of the mouth superolaterally.
  • Zygomaticus Minor: Also originates from the zygomatic bone and it helps to elevat the upper lip, exposing the maxillary (upper) teeth.

Muscles of Mastication

  • Lateral Pterygoid: Depresses and protrudes the mandible, as well as side to side movement of the mandible.
  • Medial Pterygoid: Elevates and protrudes the mandible.
  • Masseter: Causes powerful elevation of the mandible, closing the mouth.
  • Temporalis: Originates from the temporal bone. It helps to elevate the mandible as well as retract it.

Muscles of the Neck

  • Digastric: Named because it has two bellies. This muscle opens the jaw when the masseter and temporalis are relaxed.
  • Mylohyoid: Originates from the mylohyoid line of the mandible. It elevates the hyoid bone and the floor of the mouth.
  • Omohyoid: The Omohyoid muscle runs from the hyoid bone to the superior border of the scapula. Omo comes from an ancient greek word that means shoulder. This muscle has two bellies. These two bellies are seperated by an intermediate tendon.
  • Sternocleidomastoid: Named because it originates from the sternum and the clavicle and it inserts on the mastoid process. It helps to turn and nod the head.
  • Stylohyoid: Helps to elevate the hyoid bone during swallowing.

Muscles Acting on the Scapula

  • Levator Scapulae: Elevates the scapula.
  • Pectoralis Minor: Helps to stabilize the shoulder as well as protract the shoulders.
  • Rhomboideus Major: Holds the scapula to the thoracic wall and helps to retract the scapula.
  • Rhomboideus Minor: Minor muscles assist their major counterpart. Therefore, this muscle also helps to keep the scapula against the thoracic wall as well as retracts the scapula.
  • Serratus Anterior: Stabilizes the scapula. Also known as the "boxer's muscle" because it is largely responsible for scapula protraction, which is what happens when you throw a punch.
  • Trapezius: Named because it forms a huge diamond shape on the upper and middle back. Rotates, retracts, elevates, and depresses the scapula.

Trunk


Muscles of the Abdominal Wall

  • External Abdominal Oblique: Helps to flex the torso and rotate it contralaterally. Oblique means a muscle sits in a slanting position, neither straight up and down or straight left to right. When trying to decide if a muscle is external or internal, look up towards the person's head, and look at the direction of the fibers pointing up. If they point up and out EXTERNALLY, then they are external muscles, if they point up INTERNALLY towards the head, then they are internal muscles.
  • Internal Abdominal Oblique: Can compress the abdomen when both sides are being contrated together (bilateral). Causes ipsilateral trunk rotation when one side is contracted (unilateral).
  • Inguinal Ligament: This ligament is a band of dense connective tissue that runs over the top of blood vessels, nerves and other soft tissues that run from inside the trunk to the lower extremity.
  • Linea Alba: Alba means "white." The linea alba is a line of white connective tissue that runs in between the rectus abdominis muscles.
  • Rectus Abdominis: Rectus means "straight." This is the big straight up and down muscle of the abdomen that is visible when somebody has a "six pack."
  • Transverse Abdominis: This muscle is deep. It is deeper than the internal abdominal obliques. This muscle runs fairly parallel to the floor in a standing person. The Transverse Abdominal muscles are important for compressing the abdominal contents.

Muscles of the Vertebral Column

  • Quadratus Lumborum: This is the deepest abdominal muscle. It is found in your lower back on either side of the lumbar spine. It is used to sit, stand, and walk and therefore it is common to have pain in this muscle.
  • Sacrospinalis (Also called the Erector Spinae): The sacrospinalis muscles or erector spinae are really a group of muscles that are found in three regions.
    • The Iliocostalis Group: These muscle arise from the common erector spinae origin that includes the sacrum, illium and low back. The fibers connect to various ribs along the posterior trunk.
    • The Longissimus Group: These fibers arise from the common erector spinae origin. Fibers also arise from various ribs and ultimately these fibers form bands of muscle that blend together to travel all the way to the posterior skull.
    • The Spinalis Group: These fibers arise and insert on the spinous processes of the vertebrae. The bands of muscle formed can be a variety of lengths. Ultimately, however the muscle tissue appears to form a pattern of muscle that extends along the length of the vertebral spinous processes.
