Connective Tissue

Read the following carefully and then watch the video at the end. Take notes as necessary to remember this lesson

General Characteristics of Connective Tissue

  • Abundant throughout the body and makes up a portion of every organ.
  • Very few cells compared to other tissues with large amounts of extracellular substance called matrix (matrix = ground substance plus various fibers).
  • Blood vessels are present (tissue is vascular) with the exceptions of cartilage and dense connective tissue.

General Functions of Connective Tissue:

  • Enclose and separate tissues and organs
  • Connect and bind tissues together
  • Support and gives structure to the body
  • Protect and cushion organs
  • Defense against disease (inflammation and immunity)
  • Storage of energy and minerals
  • Repair of damaged tissue
  • Transportation of nutrients, gases, and wastes

The cells of connective tissue form the matrix they reside in. There are two common suffixes used to identify these cells: -blast signifies a younger cell that is actively producing new matrix, while -cyte indicates a mature cell that maintains the matrix. There are three prefixes used with these suffixes depending on where the cell is found: fibro- refers to a cell in fibrous tissue, chondro- refers to cells in cartilage, and osteo- refers to cells in bone. Some other cell types found in connective tissue that we will discuss are adipocytes, mast cells, and blood cells. The matrix of connective tissue contains three different types of fibers: collagen, reticular, and elastic. Collagen fibers are made from the protein collagen and form strong, ropelike bands that are flexible but do not stretch. Reticular fibers are also made from the protein collagen. They form mesh structures that fill the spaces between tissues and organs. Elastic fibers are made of the protein elastin and will return to their original shape after being stretched or compressed. These fibers and the cells mentioned above are suspended in a ground substance made of various different molecules depending on the tissue type.

Loose Connective Tissue

  1. Loose Fibrous Connective Tissue (areolar connective tissue):

    This tissue is often lacy in appearance and has numerous fluid-filled spaces. It constitutes the loose packing material in many organs and helps provide support and nourishment due to its vascularity. Loose fibrous connective tissue connects the skin to underlying structures and is composed of collagen, reticular, and elastic fibers as well as a variety of cells including fibroblasts and mast cells. It can be found directly under all epithelial tissue where its appearance can be quite variable. It also surrounds nerves and blood vessels and forms packing between glands and muscles.

  2. Adipose Tissue:

    Adipose tissue is widely distributed throughout the body. Unlike other connective tissues, it has very little extracellular matrix. Adipose tissue is characterized by large, round, open spaces separated by very thin plasma membranes. Adipose cells (adipocytes) contain a large lipid droplet inside them that pushes the nucleus and other organelles of the cell up against the plasma membrane. This droplet dissolves away during preparation of the slides, making adipocytes appear clear and open. The nucleus is usually visible in most of the cells. Its small and flattened appearance resembles the nuclei of simple squamous epithelium. Adipose tissue is one of the easier tissues to identify because it characteristically looks much like a hair net. Most of our adipose tissue is found in the subcutaneous areas. It can also be observed in the mesenteries, renal pelvis, mammary glands, around the kidneys, and attached to the surface of the colon. It functions as a packing material, thermal insulator, energy storage, and padding for organs.

Dense Connective Tissue

  1. Dense Fibrous Connective Tissue Regularly Arranged:

    This tissue is composed primarily of very large collagen fibers that are tightly packed in parallel bundles. Scattered among the parallel collagen fibers are the elongated, sliver-like nuclei of the fibroblasts that run parallel to the direction of the fibers. Dense regular connective tissue has a very poor blood supply. Consequently, when it is damaged, it takes a long time to heal. Dense regularly arranged fibrous connective tissue is found forming tendons and ligaments. Its function is to provide strong, flexible support.

  2. Dense Fibrous Connective Tissue Irregularly Arranged:

    Like regularly arranged connective tissue, irregularly arranged connective tissue is also composed of large collagen fibers. In this tissue, however, the fibers are not all parallel, but run in several different directions. In addition, nuclei of the fibroblasts are more oval shaped. This tissue is located in the dermis of the skin, the outer coverings of body tubes, and the fibrous capsules of organs and joints. Its function is to provide tensile strength that can withstand stretching in all directions.

Cartilage

Cartilage is composed of cartilage cells called chondrocytes located in small open spaces called lacunae (lacuna, singular). Between these lacunae is an extensive and relatively rigid matrix made by the chondrocytes. Cartilage is second only to bone as the firmest tissue in the body. The surface of almost all cartilage is surrounded by a layer of dense irregular connective tissue called the perichondrium. Cartilage cells called chondroblasts arise from this perichondrium and secrete the matrix. Once the chondroblasts have completely surrounded themselves with the matrix, they are called chondrocytes. Like dense regularly arranged fibrous connective tissue, cartilage has a poor blood supply and thus heals slowly when damaged. There are three types of cartilage based on the makeup of the matrix: hyaline cartilage, fibrocartilage, and elastic cartilage. You will learn to identify hyaline and elastic cartilage, but we will not ask you to identify fibrocartilage.

  1. Hyaline Cartilage:

    The matrix of hyaline cartilage contains roughly equal amounts of collagen fibers and the ground substance proteoglycan. Since the collagen fibers are evenly distributed throughout the matrix, it generally appears as a homogenous material. Chondrocytes can be found within lacunae, which are small open spaces present throughout the cartilage matrix. Notice the perichondrium along the edge of this tissue that is composed mainly of dense regularly arranged connective tissue and contains immature chondrocytes known as chondroblasts. Hyaline cartilage is found in the growth plates of growing long bones. It also provides rigidity and flexibility in the rings of the trachea, bronchi, costal cartilage of the ribs, and the nose. This cartilage also covers the articulating surfaces of joints so they can move smoothly against one another. It also forms the embryonic skeleton before it is replaced by bone in embryo.

  2. Elastic Cartilage:

    This tissue looks much like hyaline cartilage. The major identifying feature of elastic cartilage is that the matrix contains large amounts of elastic fibers that appear as dark strings or webbing that disrupt the smooth-looking matrix. Elastic cartilage is found in the ears, epiglottis, and auditory tubes. Its properties allow it to provide rigidity with more flexibility than hyaline cartilage.

Solid Connective Tissue

Bone is classified as a connective tissue. Like most connective tissue, it has more matrix than cellular component. In this case, the matrix is a calcified solid material surrounding collagen fibers. Under the microscope, you will see many structures and openings within the solid matrix of the bone that are essential to allow the bone tissue to live. Bone cells known as osteoclasts reside within holes in the matrix called lacunae that are similar to the lacunae of cartilage. The function of bone is to provide support, enable movement, and protect the body. Two types of bone exist within the human body: compact bone, which is more solid, and cancellous bone, which has spaces and resembles a sponge. You will learn how to identify compact bone.

  1. Compact Bone:

    Compact bone is composed of small units called osteons or Haversian systems. Under the microscope these systems have the appearance of targets with a large bullseye in the center. The bullseye is formed by the central or Haversian canal. In living bone, blood vessels pass through these canals. Surrounding the central canal are a series of concentric rings of matrix called concentric lamellae (lamella, singular). At the borders between the lamellae are lacuanae (lacuna, singular), and within the lacunae are the cells that maintain the matrix, the osteocytes. If you look closely under high magnification, you can observe small, crack-like structures radiating out from the lacunae and interconnecting the lacunae of the different lamellae. These small tubes, called canaliculi, allow nutrients and wastes to pass between the osteocytes and the blood. If the bone is cut just right, you can see structures that connect one Haversian canal to another; these are the Volkmann canals. Compact bone is found in all bones of the body.

Fluid Connective Tissue

  1. Blood:

    Blood cells are found in a fluid matrix called plasma. Blood is a bit unusual among the connective tissues because it flows and the matrix is a liquid. Cells in other connective tissues are more or less stationary and do not move around much. The matrix is also unique in that it most of it is produced by cells of other tissues, not the blood cells. While much of the blood cells remain in blood vessels, many move freely in and out of the blood vessels. The function of blood is to transport oxygen, carbon dioxide, nutrients, waste, and other substances. It also protects the body from infection and plays a role in temperature regulation.
