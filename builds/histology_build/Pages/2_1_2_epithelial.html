Epithelial Tissue

Read the following carefully and then watch the video at the end. Take notes as necessary to remember this lesson on Epithelial Tissue

General Characteristics of Epithelial Tissues

  • Composed almost entirely of close-packed cells with very little extracellular material. The cells are arranged in this dense fashion because they usually form a barrier.
  • Have a free surface (called the apical surface) which is exposed to the body exterior (skin) or the cavity of internal organs (such lining the blood vessels, heart, digestive and respiratory system passageways, body cavities, and covering internal organs).
  • Have a basal surface connected to a basement membrane composed of glycoprotein material and collagen fibers. The basement membrane anchors the basal surface of epithelium to the underlying connective tissue.
  • Do not have blood vessels (are avascular). Epithelial tissues receive their oxygen and nutrients by diffusion from underlying vascular loose connective tissue.
  • Cells are held together by specialized contacts including tight junctions and desmosomes.

General Functions of Epithelial Tissues:

  • Protect underlying structures.
  • Act as a barrier to prevent movement of many substances through the epithelial layer such as microorganisms and water.
  • Permit movement of some substances through the epithelial layer such as oxygen and carbon dioxide.
  • Secretion of substances such as mucous, sweat, and digestive enzymes.
  • Absorption of substances such as nutrients in the digestive system.

Epithelial tissue is classified according to the shape of the superficial epithelial cell and the number of cell layers. There are three types of epithelia based on the shape of cells:

  1. Squamous cells are flat.
  2. Cuboidal cells are shaped like cubes.
  3. Columnar cells resemble columns because they are taller than they are wide.

There are also three major classes of epithelium based on the number of cell layers:

  1. Simple epithelium is composed of only one layer.
  2. Stratified epithelium is composed of more than one layer.
  3. Pseudostratified is a special type of simple epithelium that is also only one layer. It appears stratified, but it is not.

Combined Classifications

Simple Epithelium Single Layer

  1. Simple Squamous Epithelium:

    This tissue is composed of a single layer of flattened cells with nuclei that resemble flattened ovals. The cells are very thin and the nuclei are often thicker than the rest of the cell and bulge into the free space, giving a cell the appearance of a fried egg. This tissue lines all blood vessels, lymphatic vessels, the chambers of the heart, makes up the alveoli of the lungs, and forms Bowmans capsule in the kidneys. Additionally, the serous membranes that line body cavities are composed of simple squamous epithelium. The function of this tissue is diffusion, filtration, some secretion, and protection against friction.

  2. Simple Cuboidal Epithelium:

    This tissue is composed of a single layer of cells that have roughly the same height as width. The most distinguishing characteristic of simple cuboidal epithelium is the large round nuclei that are typically located near the center of the cell. This tissue often forms ducts or tubes. When a tube is viewed in cross-section, the round nuclei are arranged in a single row around the circumference of the tube. When viewed in in longitudinal section, the nuclei resemble a string of beads. Simple cuboidal epithelium is found forming the tubules of the kidneys (where it participates in selective transport), exocrine glands and their ducts (where it participates in secretion), the choroid plexus of the brain (secretion again), and on the surfaces of the ovaries.

  3. Simple Columnar Epithelium:

    This tissue is composed of a single layer of cells that are taller than they are wide. The nuclei reflect this shape by resembling elongated ovals that run perpendicular to the surface of the tissue. Typically, the nuclei are located closer to the basal surface in the lower half of the cell. Another common feature of simple columnar epithelium is the presence of specialized, mucous-secreting cells known as goblet cells. These cells are easily identified because they are much lighter in color and frequently break the otherwise continuous arrangement of columnar nuclei. Under higher magnification, you will be able to see the presence of microvilli (brush border) on the apical surface of some columnar cells. Microvilli are finger-like projections of the plasma membrane that greatly increase the apical surface area. In addition, some simple columnar epithelium possess another cell apical surface modification known as cilia. Cilia are thread-like projections capable of wave-like motion that assist in propelling substances over the surface of the cells. They are longer than microvilli and have a distinct frayed and hair-like appearance. Depending on the magnification and quality of an image, it may be difficult to differentiate cilia from microvilli on simple columnar epithelium. You will only be tested on images where the difference is clear. Simple columnar epithelium is found throughout the digestive system lining the chambers of the stomach, small intestines, and large intestines. There it plays a role in secretion and absorption. It is additionally found lining the chambers of the uterus, uterine tubes, gall bladder, and bile ducts. Simple columnar epithelium with cilia helps move particles out of the bronchioles and helps move oocytes through uterine tubes.

  4. Pseudostratified Columnar Epithelium:

    Pseudostratified columnar epithelium: Although it looks like it is multi-layered, pseudostratified columnar epithelium is actually only a single layer of cells. The pseudostratified appearance occurs because although each cell is connected to the basement membrane, not all the cells project all the way to the free surface. The shorter cells that do not reach to the apical surface are wedge shaped with their nuclei near the basement membrane. The taller cells that do go all the way to the apical surface have nuclei located higher in the tissue. The overall effect is that the several layers of nuclei give the tissue a stratified appearance. Like simple columnar epithelium, pseudostratified columnar epithelium typically contains goblet cells and in the images for BIO264 will always have cilia. Because the pseudostratified columnar epithelium you will see will always has cilia, if you observe microvilli on columnar epithelium and the nuclei in a row, you can be doubly sure it is not pseudostratified columnar epithelium. This tissue is found lining the nasal cavity, nasal sinuses, auditory tubes, trachea, and the bronchi of the lungs. Its function is to synthesize and secrete mucus onto the apical surface and move the mucus and particles it may trap along the free surface and out of passages.

Stratified Epithelium Multiple Layers

*It is important to note that stratified epithelia are named based on the shape of the cells on the apical surface.

  1. Keratinized Stratified Squamous Epithelium:

    As the name implies, this tissue is composed of squamous-type cells that are stacked on top of one another to form a multi-layered tissue. Keratinized stratified squamous epithelium is characterized by the presence of a keratinized layer at the apical surface composed of dead, squamous shaped cells that are filled with the tough protein keratin. Keratinized stratified squamous epithelium is found in the skin. As you examine this tissue, you will see that at the basal surface the cells are more columnar shaped and then transition as they move toward the surface to cuboidal and finally become squamous shaped cells. This tissue is referred to as squamous because the cells on the apical surface are squamous. Another distinct characteristic of keratinized stratified squamous epithelium is its wavy basement membrane. This waviness is formed by projections of the underlying dermis of the skin called papillae that protrude into the epithelium of the epidermis. This tissue functions as a protection against abrasion, a barrier against infection, and reduces water loss from the body.

  2. Non-Keratinized Stratified Squamous Epithelium:

    This tissue looks very much like keratinized stratified squamous with the exception that it lacks the keratinized layer. One key feature of non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium is that the lowest layer of cells stains darker and as you move upward towards the apical surface the color become gradually lighter. This tissue is found lining the mouth, throat, esophagus, vagina, anus, and cornea. It also functions as protection against abrasion, a barrier against infection, and reduces water loss from the body.

  3. Transitional Epithelium:

    Although this is a stratified epithelium, we do not include the term stratified in the name. Transitional epithelium is a special type of stratified epithelium found in the urinary bladder and some of the other structures of the urinary system. It is called transitional epithelium because it can have two different appearances depending on the state of the organ it lines. For example, when the urinary bladder is in its empty or relaxed state, the epithelium will appear like stratified cuboidal epithelium with round nuclei. When it is filled or stretched, it will appear more like stratified squamous epithelium with flattened nuclei. All of the images you will see of this tissue are when it is in the relaxed state. To identify it, in addition to stratification and round nuclei look for the characteristic brick-like cells on the free surface that will cover 2-3 of the cells below them and often bulge into the lumen. Transitional epithelium is found lining the ureters, urinary bladder, and the superior urethra. It functions to accommodate fluctuations in volume and protects against the acidic urine.
