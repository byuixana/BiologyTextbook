General Characteristics of Muscle

  • Elongated cells in direction of contraction.
  • Appearance depends on direction of cut (longitudinal or cross section).
  • Three types: skeletal, smooth, and cardiac

General Functions of Muscle:

  • Contracts in response to a stimulus to generate movement
  • Movement of one body part with respect to another
  • Movement of materials through the body
  • Movement of the body through space (locomotion)

Skeletal Muscle

Skeletal muscle makes up the majority of the tissue that we think of when we picture muscles. It is under voluntary control and is located throughout the body. Skeletal muscle is generally attached to bones by tendons and uses these bones as levers to accomplish work. However, not all skeletal muscles are anchored to bone. Some, like those in the face, are attached to fibrous connective tissue. Skeletal muscle cells or fibers are very large and can be several centimeters long, often running the entire length of the muscle. When referring to muscles we use the terms cell and fiber interchangeably. This is not to be confused with the fibers found in the matrix of connective tissue. Each skeletal muscle cell or fiber is elongated and has multiple nuclei that are located on the periphery of the cell. Skeletal muscle is also striated with alternating light and dark bands running the entire length of the cell. These striations are caused by the regular overlapping arrangement of the muscle proteins actin and myosin. The dark band is called the A band and the light band is the I band. If you look carefully, you will be able to identify a very thin, dark line running down the middle of the I Band. This represents the backbone of the actin molecule and is called the Z disk. When we look at skeletal muscle in cross section, we see that individual skeletal muscle fibers are surrounded by a thin layer of loose fibrous (areolar) connective tissue, the endomysium. Furthermore, we see that the muscle fibers are not uniformly distributed throughout the muscle. Instead, they are grouped into bundles called fasciculi with each fasciculus likewise surrounded by heavier connective tissue, the perimysium. Finally, the entire muscle is surrounded by a sheath of dense fibrous connective tissue called the epimysium. The large collagen fibers in the epimysium are continuous with those of the tendon that attaches the muscle to bone. Your biceps, for example, is composed of many fasciculi bundled together with each fasciculus being composed of many muscle fibers.


Smooth Muscle

Smooth muscle is an involuntary, non-striated muscle. Smooth muscle cells are fusiform in shape (football shaped) and taper at each end. They are much smaller than skeletal muscle cells. Each smooth muscle cell contains one centrally located, cigar-shaped nucleus with a speckled appearance. The cells are arranged side by side in sheet-like layers. Smooth muscle is mostly found in the walls of hollow organs like arteries, veins, the stomach, the small and large intestines, the uterus, uterine tubes, the urinary bladder, and the gall bladder. It is also found in the eyes and skin. Smooth muscle primarily regulates the size of organs and pushes substances through tubes. Most of the digestive tract has smooth muscle arranged in two layers: an inner circular and an outer longitudinal layer. The stomach is the exception; it has three layers of smooth muscle in its walls. Because of this layering, we can often observe smooth muscle longitudinally and in cross section on the same slide.


Cardiac Muscle

Cardiac muscle is found only in the heart. Like smooth muscle, cardiac muscle is involuntary. However, unlike smooth muscle, cardiac muscle is striated. Cardiac muscle fibers resemble skeletal muscle, but with the following exceptions: the cells are branched, they have a single, oval, centrally located nucleus, and the striations are not as pronounced. Additionally, cardiac muscle fibers are shorter and are connected end-to- end. At these points of connection there are specializations called intercalated discs that both hold the cells together with desmosomes and allow communication between the cells via gap junctions. This communication allows the heart to beat as a unit. Under the microscope, the intercalated discs look like very pronounced striations that span the entire width of the cell.
