Joints & Movements

In this section we will learn the different types of joints in the body and the movements that occur at some of these joints. After you study the description of these joints you can see pictures of them by drilling down in the menus using the "+" icons in 6.1.5.

When you do the movements, you will see animations demonstrating the movement for you. When you see Abduction and Adduction, notice buttons at the top to switch between arm and hand as the hand can be a little different in how abduction actually looks.


Joints


Cartilaginous Joints

Cartilaginous joints are joints which connect two bones together with cartilage. A cartilaginous joint can be called a synchondrosis or symphysis joint depending on the type of cartilage it contains.

  • Synchondrosis Joints: Synchondrosis joints are made up of two bones connected by hyaline cartilage. These joints are stationary in nature and allow very little to no movement. Some examples of synchondrosis joints are the epiphyseal growth plates and the sternocostal joint between the first rib and the sternum. Most synchondroses such as the epiphyseal growth plates are converted to bone before an individual reaches adulthood. However, the sternocostal joint is an example of a joint which remains in its original state throughout the lifespan.
  • Symphysis Joints: Symphysis joints are composed of two bones united by fibrocartilage. Some examples of symphysis joints include the pubic symphysis and the intervertebral disks. Symphysis joints are mostly stationary. However, they allow for more movement than synchondrosis joints because fibrocartilage is more pliable than hyaline cartilage.

Fibrous Joints

Fibrous joints are joints which connect two bones together with fibrous connective tissue. These joints are stationary in nature and allow very little movement to transpire. Fibrous joints do not contain a joint cavity. The three types of fibrous joints are sutures, syndesmoses, and gomphoses.

  • Sutures: Sutures are rigid joints which connect the bones of the skull together. The fibrous connective tissue that makes up the suture is covered by periosteum that extends from the bones themselves. Some examples of sutures include the coronal, squamous, and lambdoid sutures.
  • Syndesmoses Joints: Syndesmoses joints are joints which connect two bones together with ligaments. Owing to the use of ligaments, syndesmoses allow for more movement and a greater distance between the bones than sutures. Some examples of syndesmoses include the tibiofibular ligaments which connect the tibia and fibula together and the radioulnar ligaments which connect the radius and ulna together.
  • Gomphosis Joints: Gomphosis joints are joints in which a peg in one bone connects to a socket in another bone. The only example of gomphoses in the human body are the joints between the teeth and sockets found the mandible and maxilla. In this case, ligaments known as periodontal ligaments connect the teeth to the bony sockets.

Synovial Joints

Synovial joints are the most common and the most mobile of the joint types. This mobility is largely due to the presence of synovial fluid which is contained within the joints. Synovial fluid serves to reduce friction. Each synovial joint connects two bones together and the end of each bone is covered with articular cartilage. This articular cartilage is hyaline cartilage which serves to decrease friction and absorb shock. Some synovial joints also contain a fibrocartilage pad known as an articular disk which resides between the two opposing bones. Some joints, particularly those of the wrist and knee, contain a special type of fibrocartilage pad known as a meniscus. The difference between a typical articular disk and a meniscus is that a meniscus has a hole in the middle so it doesn’t fully divide a joint cavity. Synovial joints are classified in three different ways based on their axial movement. Uniaxial synovial joints allow movement on one axis. Biaxial synovial joints allow movement on two axes which are located perpendicular to one another. Finally, multiaxial synovial joints allow movement around several axes. There are 6 different types of synovial joints.

  • Ball and Socket Joints: A ball and socket joint is a multiaxial joint composed of a ball-like end of one bone and the socket, or depression, on the end of another bone. Ball and socket joints are classified as multiaxial joints because they allow rotation as well as motion in many different directions. Some examples of ball and socket joints include the joints of the hip and shoulder.
  • Ellipsoid (condyloid) Joints: Ellipsoid joints are a special type of ball and socket joint in which the ball joint more closely resembles an oval than a sphere. Ellipsoid joint range of motion is much more restricted than that of a typical ball and socket joint. This results in a biaxial classification. Some examples of ellipsoid joints include the radiocarpal joint of the wrist, the metatarsophalangeal joint between the metatarsals and the phalanges, and the metacarpophalangeal joint between the metacarpals and the phalanges.
  • Hinge Joints: A hinge joint is a type of joint in which the convex surface of one bone articulates with the concave surface of another bone to from a hinge. Hinge joints are classified as uniaxial joints. Some examples of hinge joints include the joints of the knees and elbows.
  • Pivot Joints: A pivot joint is a type of uniaxial joint which only allows movement (rotation) around a single point. A pivot joint is formed when the cylindrical end of a bone is contained within a circle of bone and ligament. The cylindrical bone is then free to rotate around this point. Some examples of pivot joints include the joint between the atlas and axis (atlantoaxial) which allows side to side movement of the head, and the joint between the radius and ulna (radioulnar joint) which allows the supination and pronation of the wrist.
  • Plane Joints (gliding joints): Plane joints exist at a joint between two bones that are mostly flat. Plane joints allow a slight gliding movement. They are classified as uniaxial due to the fact that they are normally attached to ligaments which restrict their movement to a single plane. Joints between the carpals of the hand or between the tarsals of the foot are considered plane joints.
  • Saddle Joints: A saddle joint is made up of two bones that have saddle-shaped ends. These ends are aligned perpendicular to one another so that they come together to form an X. Saddle joints are classified as biaxial joints. A good example of a saddle joint is the carpometacarpal joint in the thumb.

Movements

  • Flexion and Extension: Flexion is a movement that decreases the angle between two body parts. An example of this would be raising your arm out directly in front of you. Extension is exactly the opposite of flextion. An example would be raising your arm directly behind you.
  • Abduction and Adduction: Abduction is the movement of a limb away from your midline. Think of that limb being abducted away from your body by an alien. Adduction is the exact opposite of abduction. Think of that limb being added back to your body.
  • Circumduction: This is the combination of movements in the order of flexion, abduction, extension, and adduction (the reverse order is also acceptable). An example of this is making a circle with your arm.
  • Dorsiflexion and Plantarflexion: Dorsiflexion is raising your toes to your nose. Plantarflextion is planting the ball of your foot to the ground.
  • Elevation and Depression: Elevation is movement in the superior direction, like shrugging your shoulders. Depression is movement in the inferior direction, like the lowering of your shoulders.
  • Inversion and Eversion: Inversion is lifting the inside of your foot up toward your midline. Eversion is lifting the outside of your foot up.
  • Lateral and Medial Excursion: Lateral excursion is the movement of the mandible away from the midline, either to the right or the left. Medial excursion is returning the mandible to midline.
  • Opposition and Reposition: Opposition is movement of the thumb that brings the tip of the thumb in contact with the tip of another digit. Reposition is returing the thumb to its anatomical position.
  • Protraction and Retraction: Protraction is the movement of a body part in the anterior direction. Retraction is the movement of a body part in the posterior direction.
  • Medial and Lateral Rotation: Medial rotation is rotational movement towards the midline, like pointing your feet inward. External rotation is the exact opposite, like pointing your feet outward.
  • Supintaion and Pronation: An example of supination is movement that turns your hand palm up, think of yourself holding a bowl of soup. Pronation would be the movement of turning your hand palm down. This is not exclusive to your hands, but that is the easiest way to think about it. If you were to think about the entire body, lying on your back would be a supinated position, while lying on your stomach would be a pronated position.
