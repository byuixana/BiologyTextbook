Bones: Arms & Trunk

In this section we will learn the bones, bone regions, and bony structures for the arm and trunk. We will ask you to be prepared to identify right and left for the majory arm and shoulder girdle bones, so it is a good idea that you prepare yourself to see the bones below and then decide if they are right or left. Admittedly, we don't ask you to know right or left for bones that are very difficult to tell like the small bones of the hand and fingers.

As has been mentioned before, it is a good idea to check out internet image searches as you read through this introduction so you can see various renditions of the bones below as you go through their descriptions. It is also helpful to make simple sketches of your own as you study these bones so you get used to their organization and where the structures are in relation to each other.


Arm Bones

Clavicle

The clavicle is the only long bone in the body that lies horizontally in anatomical position. This bone acts as a strut to connect the scapula to the sternum. To identify right and left, turn the clavicle so the conoid process is both inferior and posterior. The acromial end always must be lateral, so by using this information you can determine if it is a right or left clavicle.

  • Acromial (Lateral) End: The acromial or lateral end of the clavicle articulates with the acromion process of the scapula.
  • Conoid Process: The conoid process is an attachment site for a ligament and is kind of "cone" shaped.
  • Sternal (Medial) End: The sternal or medial end of the clavicle articulates with the manubrium of the sternum.

Scapula

The scapula is a flat bone easily identified on the back of the upper trunk. It is thought that the word scapula comes from the Greek word skaptein which means to dig. In early times, anatomists may have thought the bone resembled a trowel or small shovel. To identify right and left, imagine that you are looking at this bone from behind a person and see the scapular spine with the glenoid cavity lateral and ready to articulate with the humerus. Ask yourself which arm (right or left) would be articulating with this scapula and you know which scapula it is.

  • Acromion Process: The acromion process (from Greek: akros, highest, omos, shoulder, plural: acromia) can be felt at the tip of the shoulder. It is an attachment site for the clavicle and muscles of the shoulder.
  • Axillary (Lateral) Border: The axillary border is the thickest border of the scapula. It starts just inferior to the glenoid cavity where there is a rough thickening called the infraglenoid tuberosity and runs down to a point called the Inferior Angle.
  • Coracoid Process: The coracoid process is an attachment site for some shoulder and arm muscles.
  • Glenoid Cavity: The glenoid cavity is where the scapula articulates with the head of the humerus.
  • Inferior Angle: This is a point on the inferior scapula where the vertebral and axillary borders meet. Interestingly, this point is generally found at the level of the 9th rib.
  • Infraspinous Fossa: This is the surface of the scapula located on the dorsal scapula just under the spine of the scapula.
  • Scapular Notch: The scapular notch allows room for the suprascapular nerve to pass over the scapula.
  • Spine: The spine of the scapula is a prominent ridge on the posterior surface of the scapula and serves as an attachment site for the trapezius and deltoid muscles.
  • Subscapular Fossa: This is the anterior surface of the scapula and is the surface sliding on the ribs and trunk.
  • Superior (Medial) Angle: This is a sharp curvature located at the junction of the superior and medial (or veterbral) borders of the scapula.
  • Superior Border: This is the edgle of the scapula that runs horizontally along the top of the triangular shaped scapula bone.
  • Supraspinous Fossa: This is the surface of the dorsal or posterior scapula that is just above the spine.
  • Vertebral (Medial) Border: This is the edge of the scapula that is closest to the vertebrae.

Humerus

The word humerus comes from the Latin term umerus which means upper arm. The humerus forms a joint with the scapula called a ball and pivot joint which gives this bone a lot of movement. To identify right and left, turn the humerus so that the trochlea is medial to the capitulum and the olecranon fossa is behind or posterior and out of your view. You are now looking at the anterior view of a humerus. Now, imagine in your mind what side of the body you would have to be on to connect the rounded head to a glenoid cavity.

  • Anatomical Neck Interestingly, the anatomical neck is the residual growth plate of a young person's humerus. This line seperates the surface of the ball or hyaline cartilage from the bone or shaft of the humerus.
  • Capitulum: The capitulum is the site where the humerus articulates with the radius.
  • Coronoid Fossa: Coronoid means shaped like a crow’s beak. The coronoid fossa allows room for the bones of the forearm to move when the arm is bent.
  • Deltoid Tuberosity: The deltoid tuberosity is the attachment site for the deltoid muscle.
  • Greater Tubercle: The greater tubercle is a site for muscles attachment.
  • Head: The head of the humerus articulates with the glenoid cavity of the scapula.
  • Intertubercular Groove: The intertubercular groove contains a tendon of the biceps brachii muscle.
  • Lateral Epicondyle: The lateral epicondyle is a point for muscles of the forearm to attach to the humerus.
  • Lesser Tubercle: The lesser tubercle is a site for muscle attachment.
  • Medial Epicondyle: The medial epicondyle is a point for the muscles of the forearm to attach to the humerus.
  • Olecranon Fossa: The olecranon fossa allows room for the bones of the forearm to move when the arm is bent.
  • Radial Fossa: The radial fossa allows room for the bones of the forearm to move when the arm is bent.
  • Surgical Neck: The surgical neck is on the upper shaft of the humerus and is so named because it is a common place for a fracture that requires surgical repair.
  • Trochlea: The trochlea is the site where the humerus articulates with the ulna. Trochlea is a word that means pulley and indeed a look at the trochlea on the inferior humerus conjures up images of the grooves in a pulley.

Radius

The radius is found paired with the ulna and together these two large bones make up the forearm. The radius got its name because it rotates around the ulna when you twist your forearm. Test questions about the identification of right and left for these two bones will include them together. To identify a right or left radius, view the radius and ulna together. Be sure the trochlear notch of the ulna is facing you. You are now looking at an anterior view. When a person is standing in anatomical position, the radius is always lateral on the side with the thumb and the ulna is always medial on the side with the pinky finger. Using this information, you can determine if it is a right or left radius and ulna.

  • Head: The head of the radius articulates with the capitulum of the humerus.
  • Neck: The radial neck is the constricted shaft of the radius just distal to the flared head of the radius.
  • Radial Tuberosity: The radial tuberosity is an attachment point for the biceps brachii muscle.
  • Styloid Process: The styloid process gets its name because it is shaped like a writing instrument called a stylus. It is an attachment site for wrist ligaments.
  • Ulnar Notch: The ulnar notch is where the radius articulates with the head of the ulna.

Ulna

The ulna is the other large bone of the forearm. Generally, the ulna is the bone we refer to when we speak of our elbow bone. We will always ask right and left questions of this bone when it is together with the radius. Use the same technique as with the radius to tell right and left for this bone.

  • Coronoid Process: Coronoid means shaped like the beak of a crow. The coronoid process articulates with the coronoid fossa of the humerus.
  • Head: The head of the ulna articulates with the radius and the carpal bones.
  • Olecranon Process: The olecranon process of the ulna is the point of the elbow. It is an attachment site for posterior arm muscles.
  • Radial Notch: The radial notch is where the radius rotates around the ulna.
  • Styloid Process: The styloid process gets its name because it is shaped like a writing instrument called a stylus. It is an attachment site for a ligament.
  • Trochlear (Semilunar) Notch: The trochlear or semilunar notch fits over the trochlea of the humerus.
  • Ulnar Tuberosity: The ulnar tuberosity is the attachment site of a ligament that joins the ulna and radius.

Carpal Bones

Carpal comes from the Latin root carpus which means wrist. The eight small carpal bones form two transverse rows. It is often easier to memorize these bones by coming up with a mnemonic device. For example, the first letters of these bones are S-L-T-P-T-T-C-H from which you can form a sentence like: “Some Lunatics Try Pilates That They Can’t Handle” You might try coming up with others on your own which you think might be helpful.

  • Scaphoid: The scaphoid is so named because it is boat shaped.
  • Lunate: The lunate is so named because it is moon shaped.
  • Triquetrum (or triquetral): The triquetrum or triquetral is so named because it has three corners.
  • Pisiform: The pisiform is so named because it is pea shaped.
  • Trapezium: The trapezium is so named because it is shaped like a geometric figure called a trapezium.
  • Trapezoid: The trapezoid is so named because it is shaped like a geometric figure called a trapezoid.
  • Capitate: The capitate is so named because it is head shaped.
  • Hamate: The hamate is so named because it has a hooked process on the palmar side.

Metacarpals

Meta- means between or in the middle. Metacarpals are bones found in the intermediate hand or between the carpal bones and the digits. The area called your palm is the anterior surface of the metacarpals. There are 5 metacarpals, one for each digit of the hand. The first metacarpal is the one that underlies the digit called the thumb and the 5th underlies the pinky finger. Be sure to refer to the metacarpal bones by number.

  • Metacarpal 1: long slender bone just proximal to the thumb and is found between the base of the thumb and the wrist.
  • Metacarpal 2: long slender bone between base of index finger and wrist.
  • Metacarpal 3: long slender bone between base of middle finger and wrist.
  • Metacarpal 4: long slender bone between base of ring finiger and wrist.
  • Metacarpal 5: long slender bone between base of pinky finger and wrist.

The digits (or phalanges)

Humans have 5 digits in each hand numbered 1-5 with digit 1 being the thumb and digit 5 being the pinky finger. These digits are composed of small bones called phalanges (the singular term being phalanx). The four fingers are made up of three phalanges. Starting at the tip, the bones that make up a finger include the distal phalanx, middle phalanx, and proximal phalanx. The thumb only has a proximal and distal phalanx, not a middle phalanx. Be sure to refer to phalange bones by number like this: the distal phalanx of the 4th digit or the proximal phalanx of the 2nd digit and so on.

  • Distal Phalanx: Tip of finger under the finger nail.
  • Middle Phalanx: Small bone between most distal knuckle and middle knuckle. The thumb does not have a middle Phalanx.
  • Proximal Phalanx: Small bone between most proximal knuckles and middle knuckle.


Trunk & Vertebrae

Sternum

The sternum helps protect the heart and lungs and is where most of the ribs attach to the anterior trunk. The sternum can be divided into 3 regions: the manubrium, the body, and the xyphoid.

  • Body: This is the middle and most large portion of the sternum. It is what we might be thinking of when we think of the breast bone.
  • Clavicular Notch: The clavicular notch is where the clavicle joins with the sternum.
  • Costal Notches: The costal notches are where the cartilage of the ribs joins with the sternum.
  • Jugular Notch: The jugular notch can be felt in the middle of the baseline of the neck between the clavicles.
  • Manubrium: This is the broad section of the top part of the sternum. It is kind of square shaped. Manubrium comes from the root to mean "hilt" like the hilt of the sword where the hand goes (Manus means hand).
  • Sternal Angle: The sternal angle is the point where the manubrium joins the body of the sternum. It can be felt on the chest as a prominence below the jugular notch.
  • Xiphoid Process: The word xiphoid comes from a Greek word that means sword shaped. The xiphoid process provides an important attachment site for muscles.

Ribs

The ribs are long curved bones which form a cage around the human thorax. For the most part humans have 24 ribs (12 pairs). There can be a little variation from this as some people can be missing a pair or have an extra pair of floating ribs. Males and females have the same number.

  • Head: The head of each rib is where it attaches to the vertebrae. The head articulates with the bodies of two adjacent vertebrae and the intervertebral disk between them.
  • Neck: The neck is between the head and tubercle of the rib.
  • Tubercle: The tubercle of the rib articulates with the transverse process of the thoracic vertebrae.
  • Angle: The angle of the rib is just lateral to the tubercle and is the area of greatest curvature.
  • Body: The body is the main part of the rib.
  • Costal Groove: The costal groove contains vessels and nerves.
  • Costal Cartilage: The costal cartilage is a hyaline cartilage extension of the ribs that attach them to the sternum. It contributes to the elasticity of the thorax walls.
  • True Ribs (7 Pairs): The seven pairs of true ribs are so named because they attach directly with their costal cartilage to the sternum.
  • False Ribs (5 Pairs): The five pairs of the false ribs are so named because they do not attach directly to the sternum. The false ribs are in two groups. Ribs 8-10 are joined by a common costal cartilage to the seventh rib which in turn attaches to the sternum.
  • Floating Ribs (2 Pairs): Ribs 11-12 are classified as false ribs but are also called the floating ribs because they do not attach to the sternum at all. Their flexibility allows the thoracic cage to expand during inspiration.

Vertebrae

The human spine generally contains 7 cervical vertebrae, 12 thoracic vertebrae, 5 lumbar vertebrae, 5 fused vertebrae that make up the sacrum, and 4 fused vertebrae that make up the coccyx. Rarely a person can have 1-2 more or less. The most common areas for an extra vertebra are in the lumbar, sacral, or coccyx region. The vertebral column performs five major functions: it supports the weight of the head and trunk, it provides an attachment site for muscles, it allows the movement of the head and trunk, it protects the spinal cord, and it allows nerves to exit the spinal cord. Vertebrae have many structures in common. Even though the structures can look quite different in different regions of the spine, we tend to use the same names.


Vertebrae - General Features

  • Body: The body is the weightbearing portion of the vertebra. Intervertebral disks are located between the bodies of the vertebrae.
  • Inferior Articular Facet: The inferior articular facet is the surface where one vertebra articulates with the superior articular facet of the vertebrae below it.
  • Inferior Articular Process: The inferior articular process of one vertebra extends toward the superior articular process of the vertebra below it. These processes increase the rigidity of the vertebral column.
  • Inferior Intervertebral Notch: The inferior intervertebral notch helps make up the intervertebral foramen.
  • Intervertebral Foramen: Each intervertebral foramen is an opening through which spinal nerves exit the spinal cord.
  • Lamina: The lamina is part of the vertebral arch.
  • Pedicle: The pedicle is part of the vertebral arch and attaches it to the body of the vertebrae.
  • Spinous Process: The spinous process is the junction of the two lamina and is an important attachment site for muscles. The spinous processes of the vertebrae can be felt as the bumps that run down the posterior spine.
  • Superior Articular Facet: The superior articular facet is the surface where one vertebra articulates with the inferior articular facet of the vertebra above it.
  • Superior Articular Process: The superior articular process of one vertebra extends toward the inferior articular process of the vertebra above it. These processes increase the rigidity of the vertebral column.
  • Superior Intervertebral Notch: The superior intervertebral notch helps make up the intervertebral foramen.
  • Transverse Process: The transverse process extends laterally between the lamina and pedicle. It is an important attachment site for muscles.
  • Vertebral Arch: The vertebral arch projects posteriorly from the body.
  • Vertebral Foramen: The vertebral foramen of adjacent vertebrae combine to form the vertebral canal for the spinal cord.

Unique Features to Cervical Vertebrae (7)

  • Atlas (C1): The atlas is the first cervical vertebrae. It holds up the head and gets its name from the character Atlas in classical mythology who held up the heavens.
  • Axis (C2): The axis is the second cervical vertebrae. It gets its name because it allows the head a considerable amount of rotation.
  • Bifid Spinous Process: The bifid spinous processes of many of the cervical vertebrae are so named because they are split in two.
  • Odontoid Process (Dens): Both dens and odontoid mean “tooth shaped.”
  • Transverse Foramen: Only cervical vertebrae have a transverse foramen.

Unique Features to Thoracic Vertebrae (12)

  • Costal Facet: The costal facet is where the tubercle of each rib articulates with the thoracic vertebrae.

Lumbar (5)

The facet joints of the lumbar vertebrae actually lie in the sagittal plane. This means the articulating facets are at a right angle to the transverse processes. The superior facets face medially, and the inferior facets face laterally. The vetebral bodies of the lumbar vertebrae are the largest of all the vertebrae. The spinous process is short and thick relative to the size of the vertebrae. acrum is a large triangular-shaped bone inferior to the lumbar vertebrae at the base of the spine. It is formed by the fusion of 5 vertebrae. Because the sacrum connects to the spine and the pelvis, it acts as a link that transfers forces from the legs to the spine and from the spine to the legs. Its thick, strong shape makes it well adapted to this job.


Sacrum

The sacrum is a large triangular-shaped bone inferior to the lumbar vertebrae at the base of the spine. It is formed by the fusion of 5 vertebrae. Because the sacrum connects to the spine and the pelvis, it acts as a link that transfers forces from the legs to the spine and from the spine to the legs. Its thick, strong shape makes it well adapted to this job.

  • Auricular Surface: The auricular surface is where the sacrum joins to the pelvic bones.
  • Median Sacral Crest: The median sacral crest is made from the fusion of the spinous processes of the first 4 sacral vertebrae.
  • Sacral Canal: The sacral canal holds the sacral nerves that have descended from the spinal cord.
  • Sacral Foramen: Each sacral foramen is an opening in the sacral canal through which the anterior and posterior branches of the spinal nerves pass. The foramina can be referred to as posterior/dorsal or anterior/ventral depending on which side of the body they are being viewed from.

Coccyx

The coccyx makes up the final segment of the vertebral column and lies just inferior to the sacrum. It is formed from the fusion of 3-5 vertebrae. Some people say that the coccyx is not necessary and is just a remnant of a tail. In reality, the coccyx is a very important attachment point for several muscles.
